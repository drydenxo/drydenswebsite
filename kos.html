K-OS


A pioneer of Canadian hip hop and a master of blurring the lines between genres, Kevin "K-OS" (pronounced 'chaos') Brereton is truly a legend of Canadian hip hop and music in general.


K-OS was raised a Jehovah's witness in a household that was ambivalent towards the idea of pursuing music as a career. Of course, this didn't stop him from following his passion anyway. (and lucky us!)


As he revealed in an interview on Tom Power's Q, K-OS initially planned to only release 2002's Exit, then promptly leave the music business. However at the time, he didn't understand how transactional the industry is, and learned the hard way that he was locked into a record deal. This may have been a blessing in disguise though, because these circumstances led to K-OS cooking up his legendary album Joyful Rebellion in 2004. The title's clever wordplay comes from K-OS' act of rebelling against the industry, and having a blast doing so.


Joyful Rebellion contains some of K-OS' most memorable tracks, such as "Man I Used To Be", "Emcee Murdah", and of course the classic "Crabbuckit". Interestingly, K-OS had a sort of prophetic reaction to "Crabbuckit" after recording it, predicting that it would go on to be a hit thanks to its mainstream appeal. K-OS also learned the importance of keping an archive of his music amidst his conflicts with record labels. K-OS once met the late icon Prince, who advised him to keep a "vault" of his music to keep to himself, rather than give everything straight to his label.


Over the span of more than two decades, K-OS has solidified his place in Canadian music history. He lives and breathes music and immersion, and deserves his place among the likes of Drake, Shad, and Kardinal Offishall as one of Canada's greatest emcees.
